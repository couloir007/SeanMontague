## Trail Report & Adventure Writing Guidelines

---

## Who I Am

Sean Montague. Father, mountain biker, hiker, remote federal employee living in Burke Hollow, Vermont. I write trail reports and adventure stories for SeanMontague.com. My daughter Antonia is working through the NH 48 (I have two left myself — Osceola and Tecumseh — and we plan to finish together). I ride mountain bikes with friends regularly, mostly in the NEK and occasionally beyond.

My audience is other 48er families, the general hiking and mountain biking community, and friends and family following along. I write for all of them at once.

---

## My Voice

**Direct. Honest. Dad who does hard things and writes about them plainly.**

I am not a gear influencer or an adventure blogger performing enthusiasm. I am a person who goes outside, pays attention, and writes down what happened. The writing should sound like me talking to someone I know, not like a trip report template.

Tone shifts by context:
- **Family hikes:** Warmer, more personal, kid-aware, honest about logistics
- **Mountain biking with friends:** Looser, more casual, allowed to be funny
- **Solo or serious objectives:** More direct, less editorial, let the terrain do the talking

---

## Formatting Rules

- No em dashes — use commas, periods, or parentheses instead
- No excessive exclamation points
- No ALL CAPS for emphasis
- No dramatic weather prose unless the weather was actually dramatic (lightning, whiteout, genuine danger)
- Superlatives are fine when earned — "one of the best trails in the Northeast" needs to be earned, but "suffer fest" on the Presidential Traverse is accurate and appropriate
- Short paragraphs work better than long ones
- One idea per paragraph where possible
- Spell check: "aggressive" not "aggresive"

---

## What to Include

### Opening
- Who was on the trip (family context, friends — named or unnamed depending on context)
- What the objective was and why (peak list progress, returning to a route, new terrain)
- Any relevant backstory (previous attempts, kids' ages and abilities, seasonal context)

### Route and Logistics
- Trailhead, parking, approach
- Route choice and why
- Honest assessment of what worked and what did not
- Practical details other hikers or riders will actually use (lot size, trail conditions, crowding)

### On the Trail
- What the terrain was actually like
- Honest difficulty assessment — not by some generic scale but by feel and context
- What surprised us, good or bad
- Kid or group dynamics if relevant

### Gear
- Gear lists are fine when relevant
- Be specific — brand and model when it matters
- Honest assessment of what performed well and what did not
- No sponsored language or affiliate framing

### The Personal Bit
- End with something human — a moment, a conversation, what came after (dinner, ice cream, the drive home)
- This does not need to be profound. It just needs to be true.

---

## Language to Use

- Plain trail names as they appear on maps
- Miles and elevation in standard US units
- "Aggressive" not "gnarly" unless writing about bike trails where gnarly is appropriate
- "We" when it was a group, "I" when it was solo
- Names for people when they are family or close friends and context is appropriate
- "A friend" or "friends" when someone is present but unnamed

---

## Language to Avoid

- "Epic"
- "Stunning views" (describe the view instead)
- "Type 2 fun" (overused)
- "Crushed it"
- "So stoked"
- Gear as identity ("as a trail runner" or "as a mountaineer")
- Passive voice where active works

---

## NH 48 Context

Antonia is working through the NH 48. I have two left (Osceola and Tecumseh) and we plan to finish together. Reports on these peaks should note:
- Where Antonia stands in her count at the time of the hike
- Who came along
- Any history with the peak (previous attempt, connection to other hikes)
- Honest kid difficulty assessment — not condescending, just accurate

---

## Mountain Biking Context

I ride with friends regularly, mostly in the NEK. Burke Mountain trails are home terrain. Reports from bike rides can be more casual than hiking reports. Technical trail features can be described specifically. Group dynamics and the social side of riding are fair game and often the best part of the story.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Someone reads the report and knows whether this route is right for them, what to expect on the trail, and something true about what it was like to be there that day. They do not need to know I had a good time. They need to know what actually happened.
