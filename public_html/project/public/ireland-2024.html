Trip Notes

Ten days, the full perimeter, and a lot of stone walls

Ireland in April is not the Ireland of summer tourism brochures. The light is low and specific — that particular Atlantic silver that makes the countryside look like it's been painted on a damp morning. We landed in Dublin on the 17th, collected the car, and drove north before the day was out.

The plan was loose: north to Sligo for Yeats country and the coast, across to the Céide Fields on the Mayo coast — the oldest field system in the world, buried under four thousand years of bog — then down through Galway, out to Inishmore on the Aran Islands, and a long southern loop through Limerick, Killarney, the Dingle Peninsula, and around to Cork before cutting inland through Cashel and ending in Kilkenny.

"There is no such thing as bad weather in Ireland, only different kinds of good light."

— overheard, a pub in Sligo

What you don't plan for is the pace of it. Ireland resists the itinerary. A conversation at a petrol station turns into a detour to a ring fort. A wrong turn on the Dingle Peninsula leads to a beach with no name on any sign. The country is small enough that you can always recover ground, which makes it easy to follow things that feel interesting and let the schedule be what it is.

The North

Sligo is underrated in a way that most Irish towns of its size are underrated — it has a distinct character, a working port feel, good pubs, and the surrounding landscape is dramatic without being performative about it. Benbulben sits behind the town like it's always been there, which it has, and the light off the Atlantic in the late afternoon is something that has clearly driven poets toward their notebooks for centuries.

The Céide Fields were, unexpectedly, one of the best things on the trip. There's almost nothing to see — which is precisely the point. An almost entirely invisible Neolithic landscape, revealed through peat probing, that you look at from a viewing platform while the wind comes off the Atlantic at considerable speed. The museum is excellent. The scale of what it represents — domesticated agriculture on the north Mayo coast five thousand years ago — is genuinely hard to absorb.

The West

Galway is the easiest place in Ireland to spend more time than you intended. We had one night and it turned into two. The Aran Islands day — ferry from Rossaveal to Inishmore — was one of those travel days that earns its place in the permanent record. Rented bicycles at the pier, rode out to Dún Aonghasa in actual April sunshine, sat on the cliff edge for longer than was sensible, rode back slowly.

Limestone terraces, wildflowers emerging from cracks in the karst, the Atlantic three hundred feet directly below. A ferry back in the early evening with Connemara hills going dark behind us. The kind of day that recalibrates what you think a good day looks like.

The South

Killarney and the Ring of Kerry in late April: unexpectedly quiet. We drove the Dingle Peninsula instead of the Ring proper, which was the right call — the Connor Pass, the Blasket Island views, the town of Dingle itself with its excellent bookshop and straightforwardly good fish. Bantry Bay in soft rain. Cork for a day and a half, which is a proper city that gets on with things. Cashel on the way back — the Rock of Cashel at golden hour with almost no other visitors — and then Kilkenny to finish, walked the entire medieval mile twice, flew home the next morning.
